Our Coffee Growers

Every coffee bean and every coffee tree has a unique story to share with you.

Brazil beans

Renowned worldwide for their exceptional quality, Brazilian coffee beans are celebrated for their rich flavor profiles, balanced acidity, and smooth texture, making them a cornerstone of the global coffee industry.

Vietnam beans

Vietnamese coffee beans are celebrated for their bold, robust flavor and distinctively low acidity, making them a popular choice for strong, full-bodied brews that captivate coffee lovers worldwide.

Colombia beans

Colombian coffee beans are cherished for their bright acidity, medium body, and rich, fruity flavors, consistently ranking among the finest in the world for their exceptional quality and unique taste.

Indonesia beans

Indonesian coffee beans are famed for their deep, earthy flavors, low acidity, and full-bodied richness, often with unique notes of chocolate, spice, and herbal undertones that reflect the diverse growing regions of the archipelago.

Ethiopia beans

Ethiopian coffee beans, often regarded as the birthplace of coffee, are prized for their vibrant acidity, floral aromas, and complex flavor profiles, featuring notes of berries, citrus, and wine-like undertones that enchant coffee connoisseurs.

One out of every three cups of coffee consumed globally is linked to Brazil, the world's largest coffee exporter. Among the top five coffee-producing countries —Brazil, Vietnam, Colombia, Indonesia, and Ethiopia— each brings unique qualities to the global coffee market. Brazil, for instance, exports about 90% high-quality Arabica beans, a staple in premium espresso blends. Similarly, Vietnam leads in robusta production, while Colombia is renowned for its smooth, balanced Arabica. Ethiopia, the birthplace of coffee, offers rich, diverse flavors, and Indonesia is famed for specialty coffees like Sumatra and Java.
While all these nations have the capacity to export roasted, value-added coffee, green coffee beans still dominate exports. Expanding processed coffee exports across these leading producers could provide consumers with greater access to their unique flavors and promote value addition within producing countries.
Each country also boasts diverse coffee-growing regions, varying in altitude, varieties, and cultivation systems. For example, Brazil's regions range from high-altitude, handpicked farms to mechanized flatlands. In contrast, Ethiopia's coffee often grows wild or semi-wild under forest canopies, while Colombia's coffee thrives on steep Andean slopes.
Supporting the processed coffee industries in these countries offers global consumers not only enhanced flavor options but also a way to empower millions of smallholder coffee producers whose livelihoods depend on the crop.

The history of coffee cultivation in Vietnam dates back to the late 19th century, during the French colonial period. Coffee was introduced by the French in the 1850s, initially in the central highlands region, which offered ideal growing conditions. By the early 20th century, the Vietnamese coffee industry began to take root, with the country slowly becoming one of the largest coffee producers in the world. In the 1980s, after the Vietnam War and during the process of economic reforms known as Đổi Mới, coffee production saw significant growth, particularly in the robusta sector.
Today, Vietnam ranks as the second-largest coffee producer globally, following Brazil. The majority of Vietnam's coffee production is robusta, which is known for its strong, bitter taste and higher caffeine content. While arabica coffee is also grown in some regions, robusta dominates the industry, especially in the highlands of Dak Lak, Lam Dong, and Gia Lai provinces. Vietnam's coffee is generally characterized by its strong, bold flavors with earthy and nutty undertones.
Vietnamese coffee is highly valued for its consistency and robust production scale, but the quality can vary. The quality of the beans is affected by factors like altitude, climate, and farming practices. The country has made strides in improving the quality of its beans, particularly arabica, as more farmers adopt sustainable farming practices and refine their processing methods. Vietnamese coffee has a reputation for being robust and functional in blends, and the country's coffee culture, especially the iconic “cà phê sữa đá” (iced coffee with condensed milk), has gained international recognition. However, there is a growing focus on quality production, with some specialty coffees now coming from more selective, high-altitude farms. These efforts are helping elevate the global perception of Vietnamese coffee, making it a noteworthy player in the specialty coffee market.

Explore Our Finest Coffees

Discover the world's best coffee varieties, each with its unique flavor and story.

Jamaican Blue Mountain

Jamaican Blue Mountain coffee is renowned for its smooth, mild flavor and lack of bitterness. Grown in the high altitudes of Jamaica's Blue Mountains, it thrives in ideal conditions, making it one of the most sought-after and expensive coffees worldwide.

Colombian Coffee

Colombia ranks among the top coffee producers worldwide, with beans known for their balanced flavors, medium body, and bright acidity. Colombian coffee offers diverse taste profiles, often featuring smooth notes of caramel, citrus, and chocolate.

Hawaiian Kona Coffee

Ethiopia, known as the birthplace of coffee, produces some of the finest beans globally. Yirgacheffe coffee is highly regarded for its floral notes, light body, and bright acidity, making it one of the most complex and sought-after coffees in the world.

Panama Geisha (Gesha)

Panama's Geisha beans, particularly from the Boquete region, are among the best in the world, known for their unique fruity and floral flavor profile. Geisha coffee is celebrated for its complexity, with notes of jasmine, tropical fruits, and honey.

Sumatra Mandheling

Sumatra coffee from Indonesia is renowned for its earthy, full-bodied flavor and low acidity. Known for its distinctive herbal and spicy notes, Sumatra beans are often processed using the wet-hulling method, resulting in a rich, bold, and unique taste.

Costa Rican Tarrazu

Coffee from Costa Rica's Tarrazu region is known for its bright acidity, smooth body, and complex flavors. Often featuring notes of citrus, chocolate, and honey, it's a favorite for those who enjoy a clean, crisp cup with a well-balanced profile.
